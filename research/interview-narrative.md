# GHP Math Semifinal Interview Narrative Guide

## Core Principle
You're not selling a product. You're sharing something you genuinely find interesting. The project is EVIDENCE of curiosity, not the accomplishment itself.

---

## How to Naturally Bring It Up

### If they ask "What have you done with math outside class?"
**Natural entry:**
"I've been really interested in how certain math concepts are hard to visualize just from equations, so I started building this interactive web thing where you can actually see what's happening when you change parameters in real time. Like, I wanted to understand [specific concept] better, and making something visual helped it click."

**Why this works:**
- Starts with the PROBLEM (visualizing concepts), not the solution (the app)
- "web thing" instead of "application" or "platform" — sounds like a 17-year-old
- Frames it as a learning tool for himself, not a teaching tool for others
- Opens naturally to follow-up questions

### If they ask "What's your most rewarding activity?"
**Natural entry:**
"There's this program I've been building that lets you mess around with [math concept] interactively. What's been cool about it is I keep finding connections I didn't see before — like when I was coding the visualization for [X], I realized it was actually related to [Y], which I'd learned separately. It's like building it forced me to really understand how the pieces fit together."

**Why this works:**
- Emphasizes LEARNING from the project, not completing it
- Shows that building revealed new math insights (the judges care about THIS)
- "program I've been building" — present progressive tense, ongoing, not finished and polished

---

## The 30-Second Version
*Use when they seem mildly interested or time is tight*

"I'm building an interactive math visualization tool — you can drag sliders and see how changing one variable affects a whole system. I used AI to help with the coding part since I'm not a programmer, but the math and the design of what to visualize, that's what I care about. It's helped me understand [specific concept] way better than just doing problem sets."

**Key elements:**
- What it is (1 sentence)
- How you built it (1 sentence, honest about AI)
- Why it matters to YOU (the insight you gained)

---

## The 2-Minute Version
*Use when they're genuinely interested and asking follow-ups*

"So I was trying to really understand [specific math concept], and I kept thinking it would make so much more sense if I could just SEE it, you know? Like, the equations made sense on paper, but I wanted to play with it.

I'm not really a coder, but I know enough Python and web basics to get by. I used AI tools — like Claude and ChatGPT — to help me write the parts I didn't know how to do, especially the interactive pieces. I'd tell it what I wanted mathematically, and it would help me figure out the code structure. But I had to understand the math deeply to even know what to ask for.

What's been really interesting is that building it forced me to think about edge cases and relationships I wouldn't have noticed otherwise. Like, when I was coding [specific example], I realized [specific mathematical insight]. That insight came from building it, not from a textbook.

I'm still working on it — there are a few more concepts I want to add — but it's become this thing where every time I learn something new in math class, I think 'could I visualize that?' And then I try."

**Key elements:**
- Origin story: curiosity-driven, not resume-driven
- Honest about AI assistance, frames it as a tool for implementation
- Specific mathematical insight gained (CRITICAL — this is what judges care about)
- Ongoing, not finished (shows sustained interest)
- Natural language throughout

## Handling the AI Question

### If they ask directly: "Did you code this yourself?"
**Good response:**
"I used AI tools to help with the coding, yeah. I'm not a strong programmer, so I'd describe what I wanted mathematically and use AI to help structure the code. But the math design — like deciding what relationships to show, what parameters matter, how to handle edge cases — that's all me. The AI can't do that part because it doesn't know what I'm trying to understand."

**Why this works:**
- Direct, honest, no hedging
- Immediately pivots to YOUR contribution (the math reasoning)
- Shows you understand the tool's limitations
- Frames AI as implementation assistant, not thinking assistant

### If they ask: "So did the AI do most of the work?"
**Good response:**
"Not the work I care about. The AI helped me translate my ideas into working code, but it couldn't tell me WHAT to build or WHY. Like, when I wanted to visualize [specific concept], I had to figure out what variables to expose, what ranges make sense, what relationships are worth showing. That's the math part. The AI just helped me write the JavaScript."

**Why this works:**
- Reframes "work" as intellectual work, not labor
- Specific example grounds it
- Confident without being defensive

### If they seem skeptical or unimpressed:
**Good response:**
"Yeah, I get that the coding itself isn't the impressive part. What I care about is that building this forced me to understand [concept] way deeper than I would have otherwise. Like, I can't just handwave over the details when I'm trying to implement something — I have to actually know how the math works. That's why I built it."

**Why this works:**
- Acknowledges their skepticism (shows social awareness)
- Redirects to learning outcome
- Shows humility and actual understanding of what matters

## Pivoting FROM Project TO Math

The project is a VEHICLE to talk about specific math concepts. Always have 2-3 specific mathematical insights ready.

### Example pivot structure:
"...so that's the project. But what really got me excited was [specific mathematical insight]. I hadn't seen that connection before, and now I can't unsee it. It made me want to dive into [related topic], which I know GHP covers."

### Example insights you could use (adapt to YOUR actual project):
- "I realized that [concept A] and [concept B] are basically the same structure, just in different contexts"
- "When you visualize it, you can see why [theorem] has to be true — it's geometrically obvious, but algebraically it seemed arbitrary"
- "I kept running into this edge case where [X] happens, and that led me down a rabbit hole about [Y]"

**The pattern:**
Project → Specific insight → Broader curiosity → Why GHP would help

## Tone Guide

### Sounds right (authentic 17-year-old with genuine interest):
- "I've been building this thing where..."
- "What's cool about it is..."
- "I realized..."
- "I wanted to understand..."
- "It made me curious about..."
- "I'm still figuring out..."
- "That's the part I found interesting"

### Sounds wrong (startup pitch / LinkedIn / college essay robot):
- "I developed a comprehensive platform..."
- "This innovative solution addresses..."
- "I leveraged cutting-edge technology..."
- "My application demonstrates..."
- "This showcases my ability to..."
- "I'm passionate about..." (saying it directly is cringe; show it instead)
- "I believe in the power of..."

### The difference:
Good tone focuses on LEARNING and CURIOSITY.
Bad tone focuses on ACHIEVEMENT and INNOVATION.

---

## What to NEVER Say

### 1. "I wanted to make math accessible to everyone"
Why it's bad: You're 17, and your web project isn't going to revolutionize math education. GHP judges will see through this. It sounds resume-paddy.

Better: "I wanted to understand this better, and building something visual helped me."

### 2. "I taught myself advanced coding"
Why it's bad: You used AI tools. Don't overstate your coding skills. Also, GHP is about MATH, not coding.

Better: "I learned enough coding to build this with help from AI tools."

### 3. "I'm really good at math" / "Math comes easily to me"
Why it's bad: Even if true, it sounds arrogant. GHP wants to see CURIOSITY, not superiority.

Better: "There are concepts I really want to understand more deeply" / "I love when something finally clicks"

### 4. "I want to use this for [career goal]"
Why it's bad: Too forward-looking. GHP judges care about your current intellectual curiosity, not your 10-year plan.

Better: "I'm not sure where this will lead, but I want to keep exploring"

### 5. "This will help other students" / "I want to give back"
Why it's bad: Unless you've actually used this with other students, it's theoretical and sounds like you're trying to impress them with altruism.

Better: Just talk about YOUR learning. That's enough.

### 6. Anything that sounds like you're DONE learning
Why it's bad: GHP is about growth potential. If you sound like you've already figured it all out, why would they select you?

Better: Always include what you're STILL curious about

## Connecting to "Why GHP?"

### The structure:
Your project → What it made you curious about → What GHP offers that you can't get elsewhere

### Example:
"Building this project made me realize how much I don't know about [specific topic area]. Like, I kept bumping into questions about [X], and I'd look it up, but the explanations online were either too basic or way over my head. GHP would give me access to people who actually work in this stuff — professors, other students who are deeper into it than me. I want to be around people who can push my thinking, not just validate what I've already done."

**Why this works:**
- Humble (acknowledges gaps in knowledge)
- Specific (not generic "I want to learn from the best")
- Forward-looking (wants challenge, not validation)
- Social (values learning from people, not just content)

### Another example:
"I've been doing this project on my own, which is cool, but it's also kind of limiting. I don't have anyone to challenge my assumptions or tell me when I'm going down a dead end. At GHP, I could actually talk through these ideas with people who care about the same stuff. That's what I'm missing right now."

**Why this works:**
- Honest about limitations of solo work
- Values intellectual community
- Shows self-awareness

## Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Judge seems uninterested in the project
**What to do:** Don't oversell it. Give the 30-second version and move on.

"Yeah, I built this visualization tool mostly to help myself understand [concept]. It's been useful. But honestly, what I've been more interested in lately is [pivot to different math topic]."

### Scenario 2: Judge seems VERY interested in the project
**What to do:** Give the 2-minute version, but still pivot to math insights, not technical details.

If they ask technical questions, answer briefly and redirect: "The technical part is honestly pretty basic — what was hard was figuring out [mathematical challenge]."

### Scenario 3: Judge asks "Is this for a competition or class?"
**What to do:** Be honest. This is for YOU.

"No, just something I wanted to do. I kept thinking it would help me understand [concept], and it did."

### Scenario 4: Judge asks "What's next for this project?"
**What to do:** Don't treat it like a startup. Treat it like a tool you'll keep tinkering with.

"I'll probably keep adding to it as I learn more. There are a few other concepts I want to visualize. But it's more for me than anything else."

## Final Prep Checklist

Before the interview, have ready:
- [ ] 2-3 specific mathematical insights you gained from building this
- [ ] 1-2 specific math topics you're curious about that GHP could help with
- [ ] A clear, honest 1-sentence explanation of how you used AI tools
- [ ] An example of something you're STILL figuring out (shows humility)
- [ ] A non-project example of math you've done (so the interview isn't only about the project)

---

## The Big Picture

The judges don't care about your project. They care about:
1. Do you genuinely love math?
2. Will you contribute to the GHP community?
3. Do you have growth potential?

Your project is evidence for #1 and #3. That's it. Don't make it bigger than it is.

The best outcome: They remember your curiosity, not your project.
