# GHP Math Semifinal Strategy: Interactive Math App Validation
**Date:** 2026-02-25
**Target:** Governor's Honors Program (Georgia) — Math Category
**Decision Framework:** GO / PIVOT / KILL

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**VERDICT: PIVOT** (with conditions)

The interactive math app is a HIGH-RISK, MEDIUM-REWARD play. It can work, but only if:
1. The student can ARTICULATE THE MATH behind it in depth
2. The project is brought up naturally as evidence of exploration, NOT as the centerpiece
3. There's a clear "math discovery story" behind why he built it
4. He has backup answers that show math passion beyond this one project

**Core Issue:** GHP judges want to see **mathematical thinking**, not technical skill. A web app risks looking like a CS project, not a math project. The "vibe coding" aspect amplifies this risk.

---

## RISK ANALYSIS

### Risk 1: Wrong Signal (CS, Not Math)
**Severity:** HIGH
**Likelihood:** 70% if the app is the main answer

GHP math interviews test for **mathematical curiosity and reasoning**. Judges want to hear about:
- What mathematical concepts fascinate you?
- What problems have you explored on your own?
- What areas of math are you excited to learn more about?

An interactive web app answers: "I can use AI tools to build software."

Unless the app is DEEPLY rooted in a specific mathematical concept (e.g., visualizing the Mandelbrot set, exploring hyperbolic geometry, demonstrating the Collatz conjecture), it reads as **engineering**, not **mathematics**.

**Mitigation:** The app must be a **visual proof** or **exploratory tool** for a specific mathematical idea. The conversation should start with "I was fascinated by [math concept], so I built a tool to explore it."

---

### Risk 2: "AI Built It For You" Perception
**Severity:** CRITICAL
**Likelihood:** 90% if he says "vibe coding" or mentions Claude/Replit

GHP judges are educators. They know what LLMs can do. If a student shows up with a polished interactive web app and says "I built this using AI-assisted coding," the immediate question is:

**"Do you actually understand the math, or did you just prompt an AI to make something impressive-looking?"**

This is FATAL for a math program interview. The entire point of GHP is to identify students with **independent intellectual drive**. Using AI to generate the artifact undermines that signal.

**What judges expect from high school seniors:**
- Handwritten proofs or explorations in a notebook
- Self-directed study of college-level math topics
- Participation in math circles, competitions, or problem-solving groups
- Reading actual math texts (not YouTube explainers)

A slick web app built with AI assistance signals: "I'm good at using tools, but I'm not doing the math."

**Mitigation:** NEVER mention AI or vibe coding. Present the app as a personal project where he "taught himself JavaScript to visualize [specific math concept]." But this creates a NEW risk: if judges ask technical questions about the code and he can't answer, it's worse than not having the project at all.

---

### Risk 3: Misaligned with Interview Format
**Severity:** MEDIUM
**Likelihood:** 60%

The semifinal is an **interview**, not a presentation. Evaluators may NOT have access to his application materials during the interview. They're assessing:
- Can this student articulate why they're passionate about math?
- Do they have genuine curiosity, or are they resume-building?
- Can they engage in mathematical conversation?

Bringing a web app to an interview is awkward unless:
1. The interviewer asks: "What have you done with math outside class?"
2. He says: "I explored [specific math topic] by building an interactive visualization"
3. The interviewer says: "Show me" (unlikely in a formal interview setting)

More likely outcome: He mentions the app, the interviewer nods politely, and then asks: "Tell me about a mathematical concept you find beautiful." If his answer is weak, the app doesn't save him.

**Mitigation:** The app is a **supporting detail**, not the main story. The main story is the math. The app is proof he explored it deeply.

## WHAT GHP JUDGES ACTUALLY WANT

From the context you provided:
- "Genuine passion, independent exploration beyond classroom, creative reasoning"
- "They want to know you have drive and devotion"
- "Why should you be selected?" and "What have you done with math outside class?"

**Strong answers:**
1. "I've been teaching myself real analysis from Rudin. I'm stuck on the proof of [specific theorem], but I love how rigorous it is compared to high school calculus."
2. "I participate in a local math circle where we work through contest problems together. Last month we spent three weeks on a combinatorics problem that had 17 different solution approaches."
3. "I read 'Gödel, Escher, Bach' and became obsessed with self-reference in mathematics. I've been exploring how that connects to computability theory."
4. "I started working through MIT's OCW linear algebra course because I wanted to understand the math behind machine learning."

**Weak answers:**
1. "I built an app." (No math content)
2. "I took AP Calculus BC and got a 5." (Classroom achievement, not independent exploration)
3. "I want to get into GHP because it'll help with college." (Resume padding signal)

The app is ONLY strong if it's the OUTPUT of a genuine mathematical exploration, not a substitute for one.

## THE RIGHT WAY TO USE THE APP (IF YOU BUILD IT)

### Scenario A: App as Evidence of Exploration
**Interview question:** "What have you done with math outside class?"

**Good answer:**
"I became fascinated by [specific math topic — e.g., chaos theory / fractals / group theory / number theory] after reading [specific book or encountering specific problem]. I wanted to explore it more deeply, so I taught myself enough programming to build an interactive visualization. The goal was to test my understanding — if I could code it, I really understood the math. The most interesting thing I discovered was [specific mathematical insight from building the project]."

**Why this works:**
- Math comes first, app is secondary
- Shows initiative (self-teaching)
- Demonstrates deep engagement (testing understanding through implementation)
- Has a specific insight (not just "I built a thing")

**Bad answer:**
"I built an interactive math app using AI-assisted coding to show my passion for math."

**Why this fails:**
- No specific math content
- Focuses on the tool (AI), not the math
- Vague claim of "passion" without evidence

---

### Scenario B: App as Conversation Starter
**Interview question:** "Tell me about a mathematical concept you find beautiful."

**Good answer:**
"I'm fascinated by [specific concept]. What I love about it is [specific mathematical property]. I built a visualization tool to explore how [parameter changes affect the system], and I discovered [unexpected behavior or insight]. It made me realize [deeper mathematical connection]."

**Why this works:**
- Leads with the math, not the app
- App is a tool for discovery, not the discovery itself
- Shows genuine curiosity (unexpected behavior = real exploration)
- Connects to broader mathematical ideas

## ALTERNATIVES (Safer Plays)

### Option 1: Focus on Independent Study
- "I've been working through [specific math textbook] on my own"
- "I attend a math circle where we explore [specific topics]"
- "I'm fascinated by [specific area of math] and have been reading papers on [specific problem]"

**Pros:** Directly addresses what judges want (self-directed learning, curiosity)
**Cons:** Requires actual preparation (reading, problem-solving, genuine engagement)

### Option 2: Problem-Solving Portfolio
- "I keep a notebook of interesting problems I've encountered and my attempts to solve them"
- "I participate in [math competitions / online problem-solving communities]"
- "I challenged myself to find 10 different proofs of the Pythagorean theorem"

**Pros:** Shows mathematical creativity and persistence
**Cons:** Less tangible than an app (but that's fine for a math interview)

### Option 3: Bring a Physical Math Artifact
- A handwritten exploration of a specific problem
- A notebook of proofs and problem-solving attempts
- A poster explaining a mathematical concept (if the interview format allows)

**Pros:** More authentic than a web app, shows raw mathematical thinking
**Cons:** Only works if the interview format allows physical materials

## THE SWEET SPOT (If He Builds the App)

### Project Criteria
1. **Math-First:** The app must visualize or explore a SPECIFIC mathematical concept (not just "cool math stuff")
2. **High School Appropriate:** Should be impressive for a senior but not suspiciously advanced
   - Good: Interactive visualization of the Collatz conjecture
   - Good: Exploring patterns in prime distributions
   - Good: Visualizing transformations in linear algebra
   - Too advanced: Implementing a numerical solver for PDEs
   - Too advanced: Machine learning model training visualization
3. **Explainable in 2 Minutes:** He must be able to explain the MATH behind it without needing the app open
4. **Has a Discovery Story:** "I built this because I wanted to test [hypothesis] and I discovered [result]"

### Technical Scope
- Single-page interactive tool (not a full app)
- Focus on ONE mathematical idea, not a collection
- Clean, simple UI (not trying to look like a commercial product)
- Hosted online (so he can pull it up on a phone if needed, but not as the main presentation)

### Example Good Projects (High School Level)
1. **Visualizing the Mandelbrot Set:** Interactive zoom, color mapping, exploring self-similarity
2. **Collatz Conjecture Explorer:** Input any number, see the sequence, explore patterns
3. **Prime Number Patterns:** Visualize Ulam spirals, Sacks spiral, prime gaps
4. **Linear Transformations:** Interactive matrix manipulation, eigenvalue visualization
5. **Probability Simulations:** Monty Hall, Birthday Paradox, Law of Large Numbers
6. **Graph Theory:** Interactive graph builder, pathfinding, coloring problems

### Example Bad Projects (Too CS-Heavy)
1. Math quiz app (testing, not exploring)
2. Calculator (utility, not mathematical insight)
3. General-purpose graphing tool (already exists, not showing specific curiosity)
4. Data visualization of real-world data (statistics, not pure math)

## FINAL VERDICT: GO, PIVOT, OR KILL?

### KILL if:
- He doesn't have a specific mathematical concept driving the project
- He plans to lead with "I used AI to build this"
- He can't explain the math in depth without the app
- He doesn't have other stories about mathematical exploration
- The interview is in less than 2 weeks (not enough time to build + internalize)

### PIVOT if:
- He has a specific math concept but the app isn't the best way to show it
- He's more excited about coding than the math itself
- He realizes the app is a distraction from what judges actually want

**Pivot to:** Bring a notebook of explorations, talk about self-study, discuss specific problems or theorems he finds fascinating

### GO if:
- He has a specific mathematical concept he's genuinely curious about
- The app is a TOOL for exploration, not the end goal
- He can explain the math in depth without mentioning AI
- He has other stories about mathematical engagement (this is NOT his only answer)
- He's prepared to answer: "Why did you build this?" with a math-first answer

## RECOMMENDED STRATEGY

### Best Case Scenario
1. **Build a simple, focused math visualization tool** (10-20 hours max)
2. **Never mention AI, vibe coding, or Replit** (if asked, "I taught myself JavaScript")
3. **Prepare the math explanation FIRST** (write out the concept, theorems, proofs)
4. **Frame it as exploration, not achievement** ("I wanted to understand [concept] more deeply")
5. **Have 3-5 other stories ready** (books, problems, math circles, self-study)
6. **Bring it up ONLY if asked** "What have you done with math outside class?"

### Backup Plan
If the app isn't done or doesn't feel authentic, SKIP IT. Talk about:
- Self-study of specific topics
- Problems he's explored independently
- Math communities he's part of
- Books or papers he's read
- Why he's excited about math

**The app is a NICE-TO-HAVE, not a MUST-HAVE.**

## BOTTOM LINE

**The app can work, but it's NOT the centerpiece.** The centerpiece is genuine mathematical curiosity. If he has that, the app is supporting evidence. If he doesn't, the app is a liability.

**The "vibe coding" aspect is a CRITICAL RISK.** Judges will question whether he actually understands the math. If he can't answer deep questions about the mathematical concept, the app hurts more than it helps.

**Safest play:** Focus on self-directed learning, problem-solving, and genuine engagement with mathematical ideas. Bring the app ONLY if it's a natural part of that story.

**Riskiest play:** Lead with "I built an AI-assisted math app to show my passion."

**Smart play:** "I've been fascinated by [specific math concept]. I explored it by [reading X, solving problems Y, and building a visualization tool Z]. The most interesting thing I learned was [specific insight]."

---

## FINAL CHECKLIST: Is This App a Good Idea?

Answer these honestly:

1. **Can he explain the math behind the app for 10 minutes without needing the app?** (YES = good sign, NO = red flag)
2. **Is the app about a SPECIFIC mathematical concept, not just "interactive math"?** (YES = proceed, NO = too vague)
3. **Did he choose the math concept FIRST, then decide to build the app?** (YES = authentic, NO = tool-driven)
4. **Can he talk about 3+ other mathematical explorations beyond this app?** (YES = balanced, NO = too reliant on one project)
5. **Does he genuinely care about the math, or is he trying to impress judges?** (Genuine = go, Impressing = kill)

**If 4-5 YES answers:** GO (with careful framing)
**If 2-3 YES answers:** PIVOT (build it but don't lead with it)
**If 0-1 YES answers:** KILL (focus on authentic math exploration instead)
